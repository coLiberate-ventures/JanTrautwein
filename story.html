Part One

The Story

Stuttgart · Childhood

A Boy in Stuttgart

Jan grew up in Stuttgart, Germany, in a middle-class family — the eldest of three brothers, with Levin and Maik coming after him. It was not a household of pressure or strictness. His parents gave him room. He could do largely what he wanted, and what he wanted, mostly, was to play.

School came easily. The more engaged he was, the easier it got, and he sailed through Schickard-Schule without ever needing the discipline that other kids had drilled into them. There were no soft drinks at home, just water and the occasional juice — but with friends came the LAN parties, whole nights of gaming and pizza and sweets, no awareness of any of it, just the pure short-term pleasure of a kid who liked to indulge. That part of him — playful, pleasure-seeking, allergic to boredom — never fully left. He would spend his adult life learning not to suppress it but to build a life where it could play without wrecking him.

What did stay constant from the start: sport, nature, adventure, and books. He read for fun and he read to grow. He valued contribution, freedom, and autonomy long before he had words for them.

University · First Startup

Physics, and the First Startup

He studied physics — a discipline that gave him a particular way of seeing: systems, first principles, the search for what is actually true beneath the appearance of things. He never took the path most physics graduates take. In fact, he never held a traditional job in his life.

Instead, after his degree, he co-founded a social startup and secured EU funding — part of a government founders' grant worth around €72,000, paid out at roughly €2,000 a month over a planned 36 months. At first it was aligned and fun. Then it wasn't. Two years in, he was burned out and depressed, and he didn't have the tools or the support system to climb back out.

So he did something that, at the time, looked like failure: he walked away mid-funding, after about eighteen months, leaving the rest of the money on the table. He was in his mid-twenties, anxious, unsure what came next. But out of that collapse came the single realization that would organize the rest of his life — happiness is fuel, not a reward. He saw that if he didn't feel more energized and alive, no business he touched would ever truly work. Happiness wasn't the prize at the finish line. It was the engine. He decided it would come first.

Age 22 · Australia

The Year That Cracked Him Open

Then came the travel. Australia for a year. A camper van. Life stripped down to the road and the landscape and a lot of time to think. Somewhere in there the spiritual awakenings began — the first cracks in the ordinary way of seeing, the first hints that the self he'd taken for granted was more fluid, more constructed, more open than he'd believed.

He became location-independent at twenty-two and never really stopped. Over the years the map filled in: Bali for a year and a half, Koh Phangan in Thailand, Brazil, Sweden — each place another layer of the same education in freedom and inner work.

Photo — Australia / camper van
Bali · Brazil · Sweden

Silence, and the Medicine

He sat his first 10-day Vipassana — ten days of silent meditation in the tradition of S.N. Goenka — and then a second. No talking, no reading, no distraction; just the mind turned back on itself until it quieted. If you know, you know.

And then the medicine. In Brazil and beyond, he worked with ayahuasca — four ceremonies in time — and added psilocybin and, most profoundly, Bufo alvarius, the 5-MeO-DMT that took him to a state he describes as pure nothingness: all possibilities at once, the quantum field, the moment where the personal self dissolves and something far larger becomes obvious. At one point he moved through a three-ceremony combination inside a single week. Later would come an 8-day darkness retreat, a tantra sangha, and a steady study of Dr. Joe Dispenza's work on the mind, neuroplasticity, and subconscious reprogramming.

None of this was spiritual tourism. It was, as he understands it now, the real work — learning to be sovereign while staying soft, to hold strong boundaries while remaining open, to heal old trauma and become genuinely more free.

AirVideo · With Family

AirVideo, and a Brother's Kickstarter

The second startup came as a co-founder alongside his brother — airvideo.com. They ran a Kickstarter that raised €120,000, with more than 2,000 people pre-ordering the product. It was a real validation, real momentum, a different chapter of building — this time with family.

Photo — AirVideo / brothers
November 2018 · The Work

Finding the Work — Coaching, 2018

In November 2018, Jan found what he'd been circling the whole time. He discovered his passion in coaching and gave himself to it fully. Everything before — the physics, the burnout, the silence, the medicine, the travel — turned out to be preparation.

Since then he has worked with over a hundred clients across the world: founders, high-level executives, actors, and others who wanted to uplevel their lives and their work. He built his practice on the principle he'd paid so dearly to learn — that happiness is fuel — and on a deeper conviction that who you are being determines the action you take, which creates the results you get. Being, then action, then results, in that order.

Photo — coaching / first clients
2022 · Lisbon

Lisbon, Diara, and Now

After five years as a digital nomad, he chose Lisbon as home — landing there around 2022. He lives there with Diara, his partner and the best life-partner he can imagine: someone with an enormous capacity to love, an absolute A-player, and his best friend. They share an apartment outside the city center and a rich community of friends and entrepreneurs.

And there are the cats — Luno, a rescued male with a history of food anxiety, and Koda, a young kitten more recently brought into the home. Jan plays padel and volleyball and frisbee and table tennis, keeps learning European Portuguese, and keeps refining a life designed so that the playful boy from Stuttgart still gets to come out and play — just without wrecking anything this time.

His long-term vision points further out still: intentional community living on land, a camper van, children raised in a nature-connected, values-aligned world. The throughline from the LAN parties to the Vipassana hall to the coaching calls has always been the same — a life that feels as good on the inside as it looks on the outside.
